Our Algorithm

You may be wondering "How common is defaulting on a mortgage?". Not common at all. In fact, only 2.2% of all mortgages ever default (see pie chart). That said, defaulting is very expensive for banks when it DOES happen. So best avoided.

The goal of our algorithm is to determine what distinguishes the 2.2% of defaulting mortgages from the rest. The problem is, from our data, there may not be a whole lot separating someone who defaulted, from someone else who didn't. In other words, constructing an algorithm that can detect ALL people who are risk of defaulting, is a close-to-impossible task. A more realistic goal is to try to detect SOME of these risky mortgage requests, so that the bank will have a tool to avoid these risky investments.

The problem with designing such an algorithm, is that you will always have to deal with misclassifications: default cases that are accidentally classified as non-risky, and (even more unfairly) perfectly safe mortgages that are misclassified as at risk of defaulting.

How common is defaulting?

In our algorithm, we opted for a more conservative algorithm, which doesn't take many risks. In other words, it correctly detects 68% of all mortgages at risk of defaulting, but at the cost of misclassifying a lot of perfectly good mortgages as at-risk of defaulting.

All of this is more easily explained looking at our pie chart here. We said earlier that, that 2.2% of all mortgages default, meaning that 97.8% of mortgages are perfectly safe investments for our bank. In our algorith, we correctly classify about a two thirds of risky defaults (1.5% of total population of mortgages). This means that roughly a third of risky mortgages (0.7% of all mortgages) effectively slip through our algorithm test. Nevertheless, our algorithm, on the whole, is good news for the bank, as we avoid giving away fair number of risky mortgages, which would lose the bank money.

Here comes the flip side to our algorithm. Yes, we save money by avoiding 68% of all risky mortgages, but at a cost: misclassifying a significant number of perfectly safe mortgages as at-risk of defaulting. More exactly 25% of ALL mortgages. Therefore, even though we're saving money by avoiding some risky mortgages, we're also losing money by not doing business with many perfectly safe mortgages. In fact, if a mortgage request is flagged as risky by our algorithm, there is only a 5.7% chance that it would default. You can see the trade-off here.

At our bank, and in many others, we have done the math, and found that we are still saving money overall by using this algorithm. This is good news for us, but less good news for you, as consumers.
